Chapter 5. Semantics of Pred1

Structure for Pred1

$\mathscr{A}$ is a structure for a first order theory $\mathcal{L}$ whose function symbol set being $\mathfrak{P}$ and predicate symbol set being $\mathfrak{F}$ if it consists of

  1. set $U$, referred as the universe

  2. for each $P$ in $\mathfrak{P}_k$, a subset $\intpr{P} \subseteq U^k$, referred as the interpretation of $P$

  3. for each $f$ in $\mathfrak{F}_k$, a function $\intpr{f} : U^k \rightarrow U$, referred as the interpretation of $f$

Recall that there are three non-inductive clauses in the BNF definition of $\mathbf{Form}$, namely $\bot$, $t = t$, and $P(t_1,\dots,t_k)$. If we think about it a little bit more, we could see that the first two clauses are just instances of the third, i.e. $\bot$ and $t = t$ are just two predicative clauses where we could treat $\bot$ as a nullary (0-ary) predicate symbol and $=$ as a binary predicate symbol! The reason we include these two clauses in the language of Pred1 is that these two predicate symbols are so fundamental in first order theories and their meanings are clearly fixed for any theory. Indeed, if we choose not to include these two clauses in the language, we have to modify our definition for predicate symbol set to require the inclusion of predicate symbols "$\bot$" and "$=$"; then also modify the definition for structure to fix $\intpr{\bot}$ to be $\emptyset$ (the empty set) and $\intpr{=}$ to be $\{ \tuple{x,x} | x \in U \}$ for the obvious reason.

Semantics of Pred1

Given the definition of structure, we can now formulate the semantics of Pred1. Recall that by saying "the semantics" of a formal language, we mean the assignment of a meaning to each sentence of the language, which in turn means, deciding the interpretation for the language. Here, for Pred1, we have formulas as the sentences of the language, so in giving its semantics, we have to determine precisely the interpretation for each formula. But in order to achieve that, we firstly need to also give the interpretation for each term, since terms are an integrated part of most formulas.

With a fixed structure, we can evaluate a term if it is closed (i.e. without free variable) according to the meaning of each function symbol to obtain an element in the universe of the structure. We write the evaluation of a term $t$ as $\intpr{t}$ (we also use the double bracket notation for the interpretation of formulas), and call the result of the evaluation the value of $t$. With variables presented, however, a term $t$ cannot simply be evaluated to a single value. Nevertheless, if each of the variables in a term $t$ are assigned with a particular element in the universe, the value of the term is determined. A particular assignment of elements in the universe to certain variables is called a valuation. In this sense, we can treat a function $U^k \rightarrow U$ the interpretation of a term with k variables, where $U$ being the universe. Therefore, we could give the inductive definition for the interpretation of terms in as below.

$-$ \begin{align*} \intprx{x_i} & = \fun{\tuple{a_1,\dots,a_n}}{a_i} \\ \intprx{f(t_1, \dots, t_k)} &= \fun{\tuple{a_1,\dots,a_n}}{ \\ & \quad\quad\quad \intpr{f}( \intprx{t_1}(\tuple{a_1,\dots,a_n}), \dots, \intprx{t_k}(\tuple{a_1,\dots,a_n}))} \end{align*} $-$

Here, the $x_{1 \dots n}$ is a shorthand for $x_1, \dots, x_n$, and it denotes the correspondence of variables to elements for an assignment. Note that even if a variable is not occurring in a term, we could treat it as an variable of the term: the assignment of its value doesn’t matter though.

Let us then consider the interpretation of a formula. With a structure in hand, just as for terms, we cannot simply evaluate a formula (i.e. to tell whether the formula is true or false) if it contains free variables. And our treatment for the variables is somewhat similar with the treatment in the case of terms. The difference is that, instead of a function, we recognize the set of valuations which make a formula evaluate to true as the interpretation of that formula. Given this strategy, we can formulate the idea as an inductive definition as below.

$-$ \begin{align*} \intprx{\bot} & = \emptyset \\ \intprx{t_1=t_2} &= \comprh{ \tuple{a_1, \dots, a_n} \in U^n}{\\ & \quad\quad\quad \intprx{t_1}(\tuple{a_1, \dots, a_n}) = \intprx{t_2}(\tuple{a_1, \dots, a_n}) } \\ \intprx{P(t_1, \dots, t_k)} &= \comprh{ \tuple{a_1, \dots, a_n} \in U^n}{\\ & \quad\quad\quad \tuple{\intprx{t_1}(\tuple{a_1, \dots, a_n}), \dots, \intprx{t_1}(\tuple{a_1, \dots, a_n})} \in \intpr{P}} \\ \intprx{\phi _1 \wedge \phi _2} & = (\intprx{\phi _1}) \cap (\intprx{\phi _2}) \\ \intprx{\phi _1 \vee \phi _2} & = (\intprx{\phi _1}) \cup (\intprx{\phi _2}) \\ \intprx{\phi _1 \rightarrow \phi _2} & = (U^n \smallsetminus \intprx{\phi _1}) \cup (\intprx{\phi _2}) \\ \intprx{\forall x \phi} & = \comprh{ \tuple{a_1, \dots, a_n} \in U^n }{\\ & \quad\quad\quad U \times \{ a _1 \} \times \cdots \times \{ a _n \} \subseteq \intpr{\phi} _{x,x _1,\dots,x _n} } \\ \intprx{\exists x \phi} & = \comprh{ \tuple{a_1, \dots, a_n} \in U^n }{\\ & \quad\quad\quad U \times \{ a _1 \} \times \cdots \times \{ a _n \} \cap \intpr{\phi} _{x,x _1,\dots,x _n} \neq \emptyset } \end{align*} $-$

Validity

We say that a formula $\phi$ is valid in a structure $\mathscr{A}$ if $\intprx{\phi} = U^n$ where $ fv(\phi) \subseteq \{ x_1, \dots, x_n \} $ in a certain structure $\mathscr{A}$, which means that $\phi$ is true regardless of the assignment of the variables. We sometime write $\intpr{\phi} = \top$ if $\phi$ is valid in $\mathscr{A}$ and $\mathscr{A}$ is clear from the context. Conversely, we sometime write $\intpr{\phi} = \bot$ if $\intprx{\phi} = \emptyset$ where $fv(\phi) \subseteq \{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$ and $\mathscr{A}$ is clear from the context. Note that a formula may be valid in one structure but invalid in another structure.

When $\phi$ is valid in $\mathscr{A}$, we also say that $\mathscr{A}$ is a model of $\phi$ and write $\mathscr{A} \models \phi$. Although according to our definition of validity, $\phi$ could contains free variables, this notation is conventionally only used when $\phi$ is closed. This notation could also be extended to a set of formulas, where $\mathscr{A} \models \Phi$ means that for all $\phi \in \Phi$, $\mathscr{A} \models \phi$. Another very common and useful (but admittedly tricky for newcomers) notation is $\Phi \models \psi$, which means for all models $\mathscr{A}$ such that $\mathscr{A} \models \Phi$, $\mathscr{A} \models \psi$. Lastly, as a shorthand, we often write $\models \phi$ for $\emptyset \models \phi$, which means that $\phi$ is valid in any structure.

Exercise 2.5.1: Prove that in any theory and under any structure of that theory, for any formula $\phi$, $\intprx{\phi \rightarrow \bot} = U^n \setminus \intprx{\phi}$.

Exercise 2.5.2 Prove that in any theory and under any structure of that theory, for any formula $\phi$ s.t. $x \in fv(\phi)$, $\intpr{\phi} = \top $ iff $ \intpr{\forall{x}\phi} = \top$.

Exercise 2.5.3 Prove that in any theory, for any formula $\phi$ and $\psi$, $\models \phi \leftrightarrow \psi$ iff under any structure of that theory, $\intpr{\phi} = \intpr{\psi}$. Hint: $\phi \leftrightarrow \psi$ is defined to be $\phi \rightarrow \psi \wedge \psi \rightarrow \phi$.
